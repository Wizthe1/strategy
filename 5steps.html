Step 2: Use “Still Preparing” to Protect Your Ratings

Core Concept: This is your built-in shield. When food isn’t ready, tapping “Still Preparing” buys you time, protects your on-time rating, and can save your entire metrics score.

What You’ll Learn:
  • How the “Still Preparing” feature works behind the scenes
  • How on-time ratings affect future order volume
  • When to use it (and when not to)
  • How to combine it with store-level psychology

What Happens If You Don’t Use It

  • Your timer keeps ticking from the moment you arrive
  • If food takes 7–10 minutes, you may be late through no fault of your own
  • System may penalize you silently (fewer good offers, hidden “shadow rating” drop)

How “Still Preparing” Actually Works

When you tap it:

  • The order timer is paused or extended
  • The customer isn’t notified you’re delayed
  • Your on-time metric is protected — DD assumes delay is on the restaurant’s end

When to Use It

  • The restaurant is obviously backed up (visible wait line)
  • You’re at Wingstop, Chipotle, Panda Express, Popeyes — known slow spots
  • The staff says “It’s going to be a few minutes”
  • You've been sitting longer than 90 seconds and see no movement

Real Dialogue Example

🔊 You: “Hey, just checking — is the order for Samantha being worked on?”
👩‍🍳 Staff: “It’ll be like 5–10 minutes still.”
✅ You: *Tap “Still Preparing.” Relax. Log the wait time.*

When NOT to Use It

  • The food is being bagged as you walk in
  • You’re using it just to avoid moving or delay your own drive
  • You’re multi-apping and already running late on another app (can get you deactivated)

Advanced Strategy: Combine It with “Leave If Too Busy”

If it’s longer than 7–10 minutes, politely cancel the order and report “Merchant too busy.” If you’ve already tapped “Still Preparing,” it’s a double-shield: they know you tried.

✅ Action Plan:
1. Always check status before waiting silently
2. Tap “Still Preparing” the moment staff mentions a delay
3. Keep a quick log of which stores delay most (track trends)
4. If delay hits 10+ mins, use “Store too busy” and move on
Step 3: Stack Smart – $6 for 1? Nah. $8–$10 for 2? Worth.

Core Concept: The smartest dashers don’t deliver more — they deliver smarter. Stacking two orders in the same run with minimal extra time or distance unlocks major gains in $/minute.

What Is a Batch?

A batch is when you’re offered two orders to pick up and deliver together. These can be:

  • 🧾 From the same restaurant (ideal)
  • 🏪 From nearby restaurants (okay)
  • 📦 From opposite sides of town (terrible — avoid)

Smart Filtering Strategy

Type Example Payout Recommended? Why
Single $6 for 4 miles ❌ Too far, too little
Batch (same restaurant) $9 for 5 miles ✅ One pickup, two drops
Batch (different zones) $10 for 9 miles ❌ Long route, high delay
Batch (tight loop) $10.50 for 6 miles ✅ Two drops in same direction

What the Algorithm Is Testing

If you haven’t made much yet, it’ll test you with two bad orders combined. They’ll show you:

  • $9.25 for 8.5 miles across town = fake value
  • Two slow pickups = wasted time

Trick: Never accept stacked orders just for the higher number. Run the time/mile math every time.

$/Minute vs $/Mile — Which Matters More?

Example comparison:

Option A:
- $6.75 payout
- 2.5 miles
- 12 minutes
→ $0.56/minute

Option B (Batch):
- $10.50 payout
- 5.2 miles
- 20 minutes
→ $0.525/minute
✅ In this case, Option A is actually better — even though B “looks bigger”

Advanced Stack Tactics

  • 🧠 Accept stacks only if they go same direction or same drop zone
  • ⏱ Ask staff “Are these both ready?” If one isn’t — skip it
  • 🚧 Don’t stack from restaurants that often delay orders
  • 🧮 Use your phone timer: 5 mins wait kills a good batch

Zone-Wise Example

You’re in a Taco Bell hotspot:

  • Offer 1: $5.75 to downtown (3.5 miles, traffic)
  • Offer 2: $9.75 to suburban loop (6 miles, no lights)
  • Stack Offer: $10.25 for both, total 7 miles

Best option? The stack — if both pickups are ready. But only if.

✅ Action Plan:
1. Accept stacks ONLY if they share a direction or drop zone
2. Always do quick math: dollars ÷ minutes = $/min
3. Never accept stacked garbage just because it’s a “bigger” number
4. Track which places consistently delay — and avoid stacking from them

Core Concept: The most efficient dashers don’t go out “just to dash.” They treat DoorDash as a profit layer stacked on top of daily life. This mindset avoids burnout and creates passive wins — even on short drives.

The Problem with Traditional Dashing

  • You open the app, turn your car on, and wait.
  • You spend 20 minutes hoping for a $5–7 order.
  • Even if you make $15/hour, you feel disappointed and drained.

Result: Your car moves. But your mindset doesn't.

The Zero-Waste Motion Rule

Ask yourself before dashing: “Am I already going somewhere?”

  • 🏋️ To the gym
  • 🏠 Driving home
  • 🛒 Running errands
  • 🧍 Picking someone up

If yes → turn on DoorDash and only accept orders that align with your route or bring free extra income.

Reframe Your Strategy

Instead of asking, “Can I make $30/hour?” ask:

  • “Can I make $8 while going somewhere I already planned?” ✅
  • “Can I get paid to drive a route I already had to?” ✅

What Is Hidden Pay Compensation?

If a merchant delays your order by more than 10–15 minutes — especially after you arrive — DoorDash has the ability to:

  • Boost your base pay retroactively
  • Offer a “wait compensation” bonus
  • Issue a one-time goodwill payment

But these aren’t automatic. You must request them — and know how to ask.

Qualifying Scenarios

  • Merchant delay exceeds 10 minutes after arrival
  • You tapped “Still Preparing” and waited patiently
  • Customer was unresponsive or delivery failed due to their issue
  • Order was canceled by the store after you arrived

Exact Scripts to Use

Use these word-for-word. They are designed to signal legitimacy without sounding aggressive:

Script 1 – Wait Delay Compensation
“Hey, I waited 20+ minutes at the store after arriving on time. The merchant admitted they hadn’t even started the order. Could you check if I’m eligible for a wait time adjustment or base pay boost?”

Script 2 – Canceled After Arrival
“Hi, I had already arrived at the store and waited before they canceled the order. I used gas and time. Can you check for compensation on this one?”

Script 3 – General Late Pay Trigger
“This order ran significantly late due to a store-side delay. I believe it affected my metrics. Can you confirm if a pay bump applies?”
