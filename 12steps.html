What Is a Batch?

A batch is when you’re offered two orders to pick up and deliver together. These can be:

  • 🧾 From the same restaurant (ideal)
  • 🏪 From nearby restaurants (okay)
  • 📦 From opposite sides of town (terrible — avoid)

Smart Filtering Strategy

Type Example Payout Recommended? Why
Single $6 for 4 miles ❌ Too far, too little
Batch (same restaurant) $9 for 5 miles ✅ One pickup, two drops
Batch (different zones) $10 for 9 miles ❌ Long route, high delay
Batch (tight loop) $10.50 for 6 miles ✅ Two drops in same direction

What the Algorithm Is Testing

If you haven’t made much yet, it’ll test you with two bad orders combined. They’ll show you:

  • $9.25 for 8.5 miles across town = fake value
  • Two slow pickups = wasted time

Trick: Never accept stacked orders just for the higher number. Run the time/mile math every time.

$/Minute vs $/Mile — Which Matters More?

Advanced Stack Tactics

  • 🧠 Accept stacks only if they go same direction or same drop zone

  • 🚧 Don’t stack from restaurants that often delay orders
  • 🧮 Use your phone timer: 5 mins wait kills a good batch

Once you’re modeled, it feeds you just above your “minimum.”

Controlled chaos = algorithm confusion = reward bump

  • Decline 10 orders in a row — then accept one 9-miler that you’d never usually take
  • Dash in a new zone with no history once a week

The system sees anomalies → pauses → recalculates → offers fresh test orders. These are usually:

  • New merchants

The Myth: “Long Distance = Low Profit”

Most drivers reject offers like this:

❌ $10.50 for 9.2 miles ✅ $7.50 for 2.8 miles

But here's the truth: the $10.50 is likely highway, smooth, and done in 13 minutes. The $7.50 may take 15–20 minutes with traffic, parking, and walking.

The Key: Time vs. Distance

Use this formula:

$/minute = Payout ÷ Total Time (incl. wait)

If an order takes 10 minutes and pays $10 → that’s $1/minute. If it’s 7 miles on the highway but takes only 12 mins, it’s often better than a 2-mile traffic mess that takes 20 mins.

How to Spot a Highway-Optimized Offer

  • Ends in a “beltline,” loop, or ring road
  • Starts from a restaurant near an on-ramp
  • Little to no stoplights or school zones on route
  • Clean delivery zones with minimal apartment clutter

Best Time for These Orders

  • 🏙 2–4 PM (post-lunch slowdown with clean roads)
  • 🌄 Early morning pre-rush hour (7–9 AM)
  • 🌙 Late night after 9:30 PM (no traffic, fast highways)

How to Use Them Strategically

  • 🔥 Use them to leave high-competition zones on your own terms
  • 🔄 Plan a return-to-hotspot with a stack or ride-back order
  • 💡 Use Gridwise or Google Timeline to analyze your fastest zones

Example Comparison
